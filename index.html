About Me

I am a researcher working at the intersection of Macroecology, Aquatic Ecology, Pollution Ecology, and Ecological Data Science. My research investigates how environmental drivers and emerging pollutants shape biodiversity patterns across aquatic and semi-aquatic ecosystems.

My work focuses on understanding how organisms interact with their environments and how both natural processes and human activities influence the distribution, traits, and ecological functioning of species. I study multiple faunal groups including macroinvertebrates, shrimp, aquatic invertebrates, birds, amphibians, and other wildlife to understand ecological responses across taxa and ecosystems.

A major component of my current research examines the ecology and movement of microplastics and other emerging pollutants across food webs. Using large ecological datasets, trait-based approaches, and statistical modeling, I aim to uncover global patterns of pollutant exposure and vulnerability among species.

More broadly, my research integrates biodiversity synthesis, ecological modeling, and data-driven analysis to understand environmental change in the Anthropocene and its consequences for ecological communities.

In addition to research, I am actively involved in teaching and mentoring students, supporting ecological data analysis, and training young researchers in scientific writing and quantitative ecology.

PhD Dissertation

My doctoral research investigated the ecological functioning of riverine macroinvertebrate communities across different mesohabitats in the Wei River Basin, Shaanxi Province, Northwest China. The work applied advanced statistical approaches, including piecewise structural equation modeling (piecewiseSEM), to understand how environmental drivers shape macroinvertebrate community patterns in river ecosystems.
